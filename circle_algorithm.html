The Circle Algorithm

Interactive companion to the grokked mod-113 network. Five pictures: the winding map, the dials that store it, the DFT that finds it, the interference that answers with it — and the whole algorithm run end-to-end in plain code, no network anywhere.

1 · Pegs and winding

113 fixed pegs. Frequency k is a gear ratio: token a lands on peg (k·a) mod 113. Because 113 is prime, every k is a bijection — no peg is ever shared. Connecting consecutive tokens draws the star polygon {113/k}.

2 · Dials and shadows (why rows are sinusoids)

The point walks the k-wound pegs. Each embedding dimension is a fixed axis (a “dial”) through the plane, with its own orientation φ and gain α. Its stored value is the point's shadow on that axis — and the shadow of circular motion is a sinusoid. Phase = the dial's orientation. Two independent dials recover the whole plane.

3 · The DFT: freeze and stack, or spin and cancel

Signal: a real cosine at frequency ksig with phase φ — i.e. a mirror-pair of unit rotators (+k and −k). The probe counter-rotates everything at kprobe. Arrows are added tip-to-tail over all 113 tokens. If the probe matches, the +k rotator freezes: 113 parallel arrows stack into a straight wall of length 113/2, pointing at the phase angle. Everything else keeps spinning and its path closes into a rosette that returns to zero.

4 · Scoring by interference (why several frequencies)

logit(c) = Σk∈S cos(2πk(a+b−c)/113). At the correct c every frequency votes +1 simultaneously; elsewhere the cosines scatter and cancel. Each added frequency sharpens the peak and widens the margin — one k is a correct algorithm, five is a confident one.

5 · The whole algorithm, end to end (no network)

Everything the trained model does, executed in plain arithmetic — pick a, b, and one frequency k, then press run. Embed: a and b land on their pegs, angle = (k·x mod 113)/113 of a turn. Add: b's angle is laid onto the end of a's; if the sum passes a full turn it simply wraps — the mod costs nothing, the circle does it. Decode, two ways that must agree: score every candidate c by cos(θsum − 2πk·c/113) and take the argmax (what the unembedding does), or "ungear" algebraically — the sum sits on peg s = k·(a+b) mod 113, so c = k−1·s mod 113 (k has an inverse because 113 is prime). One frequency is already a complete, correct algorithm.
